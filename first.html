DEEP FAKE FACE DETECTION

ABSTRACT


The rise of deep fake videos, generated using artificial intelligence techniques to create realistic yet fabricated content, poses a growing threat to the authenticity of digital media. These manipulated videos have the potential to spread misinformation, erode trust in visual content, and disrupt societal norms. To address this challenge, we propose an innovative approach for detecting deep fake videos based on Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks, a class of recurrent neural networks (RNNs) renowned for their ability to learn from sequential data. By focusing on temporal relationships within video frames, our method captures the dynamic aspects of video sequences, enabling effective identification of inconsistencies that often characterize deep fake content, such as unnatural facial expressions or irregular eye movements. In our approach, we leverage a combination of pre-processing techniques, including optical flow computation and facial landmark tracking, to enhance frame-level analysis. This allows the LSTM network to focus on subtle, temporal patterns and relationships between successive frames. We further improve the detection process by employing an ensemble of LSTM models, integrated with other machine learning algorithms, to optimize both accuracy and robustness. Experiments conducted on a large dataset of both known and previously unseen deep fake videos demonstrate the superior performance of our LSTM-based system, with high detection accuracy and a low rate of false positives. When compared to existing methods, our approach shows clear advantages in terms of generalization and resilience to evolving deep fake techniques. The findings highlight the potential of LSTM-based models as a promising solution to mitigate the harmful impact of deep fakes on public trust and media integrity. As the technology behind deep fake videos continues to advance, our research contributes to the development of more effective tools for safeguarding digital media authenticity and countering the spread of deceptive multimedia.
